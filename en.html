Identity and Context

The Dante Fountain is a bronze work by sculptor Max Faller. It was installed in May 1965, the year of Dante’s 700th birthday, in the garden courtyard of the LMU building at Veterinärstraße 1. The relief program draws on the Divina Commedia and arranges Inferno, Purgatorio and Paradiso as a vertical visual journey.

Method

The cylindrical surface is conceptually unfolded into a 360-degree view. Scene identifications remain scholarly working hypotheses. The earlier excursus on Coriolanus and Dede Korkut has been removed from this project version so that the documentation remains focused on the fountain and its Dante iconography.

Auerbach Perspective

Auerbach / Figura / Mimesis: figural reality, stylistic mixture and the tension between crowd and individual provide a useful frame for Faller's sculptural reading of Dante.

Comparative Dante Iconography

The following selection presents a few public-domain or freely licensed comparison images. It is not intended as a full inventory, but as a visual guide to the position of Faller’s fountain.

Domenico di Michelino: Dante and the Three Realms (1465)

An early Renaissance image linking Dante, the city, the book and the landscape of the afterlife.

Sandro Botticelli: Map of Hell

Botticelli translates Dante’s Hell into a precise topographical order.

Gustave Doré: Paolo and Francesca

Doré’s Inferno illustrations strongly shaped the modern visual imagination of Dante’s Hell.
